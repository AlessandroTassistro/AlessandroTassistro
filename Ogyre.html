Ogyre: empowering corporate engagement and consumer participation for ocean cleanup

THE PROBLEM
For my thesis at IAAD (Master in Digital Communication and Marketing), I chose to tackle a project that aims to improve the physical world using digital tools. In particular, I was challenged by my professor to create a solution to marine littering that generates an emotional response from the everyday user.

The first step in my project was to fully understand the problem at hand. I conducted thorough research and discovered the major types of trash contributing to marine littering. Among the results, one kind of trash was shown to be the most widespread: microplastics. These tiny plastic particles, less than 5 millimeters in size, originate from the breakdown of larger plastics and synthetic products. Microplastics permeate marine ecosystems and pose significant risks to marine life and human health by entering the food chain.

According to data from conservation.org, twelve million metric tons of plastic find their way into our oceans every year, posing a critical threat to marine ecosystems. How can we transform the concept of 'collecting a specific quantity of rubbish' into a clear, engaging message that not only highlights their commitment but also resonates with their audience? 
THE CONTEXT
The project is designed to thrive in a time where technology profoundly impacts the lives of consumers. To better understand how this new kind of world could influence the project, I analyzed numerous fields. The two most interesting areas were how ecology and money collecting have evolved. Here are reported the insights I developed for these two subjects:

The digital world has changed money collecting in various way:
↓ Emotional connections
Digital technology has revolutionized charitable giving by enabling charities to create emotionally resonant, narrative-driven campaigns through digital platforms. These platforms allow organizations like Friends of the Earth to use vivid imagery and compelling stories to depict the urgent realities of climate change, motivating donors to take action.
↓ Personal involvement and microdonations
Technology has personalized the donation process, allowing donors to engage with campaigns in real time from anywhere. This accessibility has led to the rise of microdonations and apps like Buycott, which enable consumers to support causes through everyday activities like shopping.
↓ Greater transparency and trust
Digital tools have enhanced transparency in how donations are used, thereby increasing donor trust and commitment. Many platforms offer direct feedback, providing donors with updates and reports on the real-time impact of their contributions, which not only demonstrates tangible results but also deepens the emotional connection to the cause.
↓ Collective action and community building
The digital age has expanded the reach of charity by creating a sense of community among donors. Social media platforms enable individuals to share their charitable activities, inspiring others to contribute and amplifying the emotional rewards of giving as they witness the collective impact.

The digital world has changed ecology in various way:
↓ Real-time data
Technological advances have enhanced real-time environmental monitoring. Tools like UNEP's GEMS Air provide instant air quality updates, while the Freshwater Ecosystem Explorer gives up-to-date assessments of global water bodies, helping to engage the public and highlight the immediate impacts of conservation efforts.
↓ Personalized Commitment
Digital tools personalize environmental conservation, enabling individuals to use apps and platforms to monitor their ecological footprint, engage in challenges, and align lifestyle choices with sustainability goals. This personalization demonstrates the tangible impact of their actions, deepening their emotional connection to the environment.
↓ Impact visualization
Technological advances enable vivid visualizations of ecological changes, helping individuals and communities grasp the impact of environmental issues. Platforms like the United Nations Biodiversity Lab 2.0 provide detailed visual data on environmental degradation and areas of natural significance, enhancing the emotional resonance of these topics.
↓ Global community and connections
Digital technology fosters global communities focused on ecological issues by enabling individuals to share experiences, solutions, and stories via social media, forums, and collaborative platforms. This network not only disseminates knowledge but also cultivates a sense of collective responsibility, enhancing personal investment in environmental health.
THE PARTNER
Given the vast scope of the problem, partnering with an established brand could significantly enhance the impact of the project. On the recommendation of my professor, I decided to collaborate with Ogyre. Ogyre is an Italian non-profit organization dedicated to addressing the critical issue of ocean pollution, with a specific focus on removing plastic waste from marine environments.

THE STRATEGY
In partnership with Ogyre, this project aims to create a digital tool specifically designed for brands to articulate and showcase their efforts in marine pollution reduction to their stakeholders and clients. The strategy involves developing a platform that translates quantitative data on waste collection into engaging, narrative-driven content that highlights each brand's commitment to environmental stewardship. This tool will be sold to brands looking to enhance their environmental responsibility profile and deepen stakeholder engagement through transparent communication.

THE TARGETS
In developing a digital tool for Ogyre that can be adopted by different brands to improve their environmental initiatives, it is crucial to start defining the primary target audience. This analysis is divided into two distinct categories: the brands (business-to-business, B2B) and the end users (prosumers, B2C) who will interact with the tool through these brands.

B2B target analysis
When developing a digital tool with Ogyre to enhance environmental initiatives, it's crucial to target specific B2B groups within industries that significantly impact ocean pollution. The focus is on sectors such as fashion and beauty, beverage companies, cigarette manufacturers, and fast food chains—each with a substantial environmental footprint and consumer reach. These industries face unique sustainability challenges and opportunities: fashion brands might use the tool to promote sustainable practices, beverage companies could align it with recycling efforts, cigarette brands might encourage proper disposal of butts, and fast food chains could advocate for sustainable packaging and cleanup participation. These strategic alignments can significantly enhance each sector's environmental efforts while engaging a broader consumer base.

B2C target analysis
When developing a digital tool in collaboration with Ogyre to promote sustainable practices and improve ocean conservation efforts, it is crucial to understand the end user, here referred to as the prosumer. This segment of the market analysis focus onto the motivations, behaviors and preferences of individual users who interact with the tool across various brands. These users are not simple consumers, but proactive participants (prosumers) whose actions and commitment determine the success of the digital tool. This analysis is based on a targeted approach, starting with a survey conducted in an environmentally focused online community (Reddit). The survey was designed to assess the current level of engagement in digital charity projects, uncover the motivations behind that engagement, and identify barriers that may prevent participation.
THE SURVEY RESULTS
THE HMW QUESTIONS
The responses from this survey were instrumental in identifying key areas where our digital tool could improve or innovate to better meet the needs of our users. Based on this feedback, I formulated a series of “How Might We” (HMW) questions. Each HMW question addresses a distinct aspect of users' interaction and engagement with the digital tool, from enhancing the feeling of active participation and offering tangible rewards, to integrating the act of donating into users' daily routines and to guarantee transparency in the use of their contributions.

How Can We Make the Act of Doing Charity Shareable?
Problem: Users feel detached from their contributions.
Insight: Users are more motivated to donate when they can see and influence the outcomes of their contributions.
HMW: How might we create a more interactive experience that allows users to see real-time results and participate in decision-making?

How Can We Make the User as a Part of the Project?
Problem: Users find it difficult to share their charitable activities with their network.
Insight: Users enjoy sharing their good deeds on social media, which can also encourage others to donate.
HMW: How might we facilitate easy sharing of charitable activities that not only highlight individual contributions but also encourage participation?

How Can We Make the Act of Donating Part of the Usual Routine?
Problem: Donating feels like a separate, sometimes burdensome task.
Insight: Users are more likely to donate regularly if the process is integrated into their activities and routines.
HMW: How might we integrate donation options into everyday digital and physical routines to make giving as habitual as checking the weather?

How Can We Make the Transparency and Impact Reporting?
Problem: Users are skeptical about where their money goes and the impact it has.
Insight: Trust is significantly enhanced when users receive detailed reports about the use and outcomes of their donations.
HMW: How might we utilize digital tools to provide real-time impact reporting that builds trust and transparency?

THE APPLIED SOLUTION
In order to better visualise the work I have decided to imagine working for an existing brand, in this case Starbucks. In this specific case the tool has been coniugated into "Rewards for Reuse Program". Each time a customer uses a reusable product purchased from the brand, the brand will donate an amount equivalent to the cost of the disposable alternative that the customer did not use. This donation will be triggered by each verified in-app purchase using the reusable product. The focal point is to lead the customers to make wise and sustainable decisions.

THE EDUCATION
Launch a social media-driven video campaign titled "One Cup, One World - Share Your Story," where users are encouraged to watch a video series, share their sustainability stories using the hashtag #OneCupOneWorldStory, and post on a dedicated campaign landing page. The campaign aims to educate and engage customers by offering incentives for participation, such as exclusive merchandise or a year's supply of coffee for impactful contributions. The ultimate goals are to build a community that understand the problem at its root, and create a viral loop of awareness and advocacy.
